Osgoode Surveillance Study

Analysis of Results

Before turning to the analysis, it is important to acknowledge that the data is not perfectly proportionate, as there were significantly more female respondents than male. That said, there are still noticeable trends within each group. For the purposes of analysis and this project, and in line with the underlying theory and research, the comparison focuses on female- and male-identifying participants.

Perception vs. Reality

One of the clearest trends is that women tend to perceive fewer cameras across most spaces, while men tend to estimate higher levels of surveillance. For example, in the library, about 58% of women estimated there were between 1-5 cameras. In contrast, men’s responses skewed higher: 27% estimated there were 6-10 cameras, and 40% estimated 11-20. Similar patterns appear in spaces such as prayer rooms and common areas. Most women believed there were no cameras in prayer rooms, while men were more evenly split across different estimates.

This raises an important point about perception. Men may be more attuned to, or expect, surveillance infrastructure. For example, one participant noted, “I thought there were a lot more cameras around.” In contrast, women may either underestimate the presence of cameras or interpret these spaces differently, reflecting beliefs that only a limited number of cameras should be present. This aligns with existing research suggesting that surveillance tools are typically designed and operated by men, where women and others are more often positioned as the subjects of observation rather than those in control (Norris & Armstrong; Zuriak pg. 50).

Comfort Levels and Privacy Concerns

When shifting from perception to comfort levels, the results become more complex. At building entrances, most participants overall indicated that they “strongly agree” they are comfortable with cameras. However, women demonstrated a much broader range of responses. Although the overall average appears similar across genders, women’s perspectives are more varied. This finding aligns with researcher Monahan’s argument that surveillance often fails to address the actual harms women experience in public spaces (Monahan, pg 287). If cameras are primarily reactive, used after an incident rather than preventing it, their presence may not translate into a meaningful sense of safety, contributing to ambivalence.

This broader range is reflected in other areas as well. For instance, approximately 28% of women did not feel strongly that cameras near stairwells or exits were acceptable, and more women than men reported that surveillance in school settings feels like an invasion of privacy. This suggests an underlying tension: while surveillance is framed as enhancing safety, it simultaneously raises concerns about privacy and bodily monitoring for many women.

Reactions to the Actual Scope of Surveillance

This tension is further evident when participants were informed of the actual number of cameras. While most participants reported “no change” in comfort levels, women’s responses tended to split in two directions: some felt slightly less comfortable, while others felt slightly more comfortable. Drawing on Stark’s theory, the decrease in comfort may be explained by the perception that the number of cameras remains excessive, suggesting a heightened level of observation and control over women’s bodies (Stark, pg 1076).

At the same time, those who reported increased comfort may interpret surveillance as providing added security, particularly in spaces perceived as vulnerable. One participant noted that:

“although privacy is important inside the rooms itself (washroom, prayer room), those areas are vulnerable to attacks. cameras that capture who comes in/outs would help.”

While not representative of all responses, this highlights that, for some women, safety concerns may justify surveillance, but only in specific and limited contexts.

Context and Normalization

For participants who reported no change, location appears to play a significant role. Cameras on campus are generally placed in expected areas, such as entrances and common spaces, rather than in private areas. As a result, surveillance may feel normalized, even if it is not necessarily welcomed.

Additionally, other forms of surveillance, such as key card access systems, were generally more accepted. Participants across genders tended to view these measures as less invasive and more focused on controlling physical access rather than monitoring behaviour.

Finally, the context of Osgoode itself may shape these perceptions. As a relatively small and professional school environment, it may foster a greater sense of safety and trust within the community. This context likely influences how surveillance technologies are perceived, particularly in comparison to more public or less familiar spaces such as city streets or large workplaces, as seen in our research.

Overall, while the data is not conclusive, the observed trends suggest that gender plays a role in shaping perceptions of surveillance, particularly in relation to privacy, safety, and trust. However, a larger and more balanced sample would be required to draw more definitive conclusions.

Methodology & Survey Design

The survey consisted of two parts, and participants were specifically current Osgoode students.

Part 1: Demographic Information

The first part collected demographic information such as gender, ethnicity, religious practice, age group, and time spent in the building. Although the main focus of the study is on gendered perspectives of surveillance, we included additional demographic questions in order to capture a wider range of interpretations and experiences.

Inclusive Design Choices

In designing this section, we incorporated ideas from the article “Designing Forms for Gender Diversity and Inclusion” to create more inclusive questions. This included making the survey anonymous, providing a “prefer not to say” option to keep responses voluntary, and including a customizable option for more complex identities. Through this approach, we were able to include two participants who identified as non-binary. While this number is too small to support meaningful analysis, their participation highlights the possibility of additional gendered perspectives on surveillance that could be explored in future research.

For the question on gender, we explicitly listed four categories: cisgender male, cisgender female, transgender male, and transgender female. We defined “women” in this study as individuals who self-identify as such, including trans women, following a similar approach used in existing research on gendered perspectives of surveillance. Accordingly, both cisgender women and transgender women were grouped under “women,” and the same approach was applied to men. While we recognize that transgender individuals may interpret surveillance differently from cisgender individuals due to their unique experiences, we adopted this approach in light of the small sample size and to reduce the complexity of interpretation.

Part 2: Perspectives on Surveillance

The second part of the survey measured participants’ perspectives on surveillance within the building through a series of questions. These included:

  • Rating their level of agreement with specific statements.
  • Estimating the number of cameras in different types of spaces.
  • Recording any changes in attitude before and after being informed of the actual number of cameras in the building.
  • Identifying the types of surveillance technologies they had noticed, such as cameras, keycard scanners, and posted monitoring signage.

Assessing Attitudes

The goal of this section was to assess participants’ overall attitudes toward surveillance. By asking a series of questions framed in different ways, we aimed to break down participants’ responses and gain deeper insight into both their conscious and unconscious attitudes toward surveillance. We intentionally limited the number of written-response questions to encourage higher participation.

Specifically, the survey included only one written-response question at the end, which invited participants to share any additional thoughts. This open-ended question was intended to capture further perspectives in case earlier sections did not fully reflect participants’ views. Two guiding questions were provided to give participants direction aligned with the goals of the study, while also offering structure for those who wished to contribute more detailed responses.

Additionally, we designed the questions to be as simple and clear as possible to reduce the effort required to complete the survey. In developing this section, we followed recommendations from this paper to make the survey more accessible and easier for all participants to complete.

Gendered Outlooks on Surveillance: Existing Research

Introduction

Defining Osgoode: Workplace, School, and Public Place - The research referenced speaks to Osgoode as a multi-purpose space.

Defining Common Terms and Trends in Gender-Surveillance Research

  • Gender: In the studies referenced, gender is self-identified and, unless indicated, categories are often binary and do not ask participants to specify sex at birth (not delineating between cisgender or trans).
  • Surveillance: The definition we adopted for our research was “technological systems that facilitate the control of people”.[i] In wider research this frequently referenced cameras, but also electronic gates and card scanners.

Major Takeaway

Women are far less comfortable and supportive of video surveillance in public spaces. Multiple theories exist as to the cause of this disparity. An overriding theory is that ”public space” is not perceived as a gender-neutral space but rather exists as a byproduct of the "masculinization of public”.[ii] Within this power dynamic, women are reticent to relinquish the rights to their data which is picked up by surveillance - such as their movement, image, and actions.

The “datafication” of surveillance that often occurs with digital surveillance does not eliminate the gendered realities of public observations.[iii] “The reduction of people and social practices to data that can be easily manipulated is an exercise of power that demands feminist critique and intervention.”[iv]

Attentiveness to surveillance is key to compliance

Michelle Foucault’s original conceptualisation of the panopticon was it fostered internally motivated compliance due to the awareness of the constant potential for observation. Modern scholars theorise that the rise in observation has led to a constant vigilance which is not experienced equally. Rather it aligns with gendered hierarchies of power, which some call “masculine control at a distance”.[v]

Surveillance in Canadian Schools

Some Canadian public schools have had physical surveillance since the 1970’s, with some school boards adopting police presence on campus as a form of surveillance.[vi] Canadian schools were frontiers in the introduction of technology and subsequently of surveillance potential: in 1999 Canada became the first country to connect all of its public schools to the internet.[vii]

Technological tools that were purported to provide students with more protection and autonomy ended up enabling student monitoring for compliance with rules such as dress codes and administrative policies.

Surveillance in universities, such as York University, is determined by individual policies but are beholden to privacy legislation such as the Freedom of Information and Protection of Privacy Act.[viii]

Outlooks on Surveillance are Gender-Specific

Women are Less Comfortable Being Surveilled

Surveillance is not implemented indiscriminately or experienced uniformly.[ix] Surveillance is often wielded by the powerful against those with less power and often amplify gender inequality.

In the workplace environment, Stark, Stanhaus and Anthony found that women were far less likely to be accepting of facial recognition technology (FRT) camera surveillance at work.

Actions and technologies which involve monitoring information, whether or not that information is derived from physical movement or online activities, is a form of surveillance.[x]

Research out of Toronto indicates that women feel that surveillance speaks to power and inequality, not just observation. Even when women had been victims of crimes, they still generally had more negative feelings of CCTV than others.[xi]

This study out of Toronto used specific groups of women to do its research: club-going, low-income, racialised, senior, sex-working women.[xii]

Possible Explanations for Gendered Responses

Lower Utility of Video Surveillance for Gender Minorities

Video surveillance is widespread and has a reduced impact as a preventative measure for crimes or incidents that disproportionately impact women. The major issues women experience in public are generally not addressed by surveillance. The preventative approach is also less than satisfactory for women who feel that surveillance itself has a cost.[xiii]

A 2013 study of Urban Security in Toronto found that amongst the 51 women who participated not one woman felt that that surveillance reduced crime while some felt that it created unsafe conditions for them.[xiv]

Women who are continually scrutinised through video cameras aren’t necessarily receiving any additional protection from gendered harms such as harassment or assault. Some common harms, such as verbal harassment, are unable to be tracked by surveillance cameras.

Women in Toronto have reported that their surveillance goals are not purely crime-related, rather “inequality and power differences, about physical well-being and health, about motherhood, sexuality, and consumerism, and about income security… control over their bodies, how their bodies are seen, and by whom.”[xv]

“The camera is not going to save us half the time and it might not do anything afterwards either...Especially when you are drunk, you know, in a place.”
– Club-going woman in Toronto[xvi]

Gendered Disadvantage in Outcomes of Surveillance

FRT & Ineffectiveness: As the effectiveness of FRT is predicated on the representativeness within the training database, accuracy is not a given. Rather, FRT has been shown to disproportionately misidentify women, and women of colour in particular.

The Gendered Shades Project out of MIT showed that amongst popular FRT databases (IBM/Microsoft):

  • All classifiers perform better on male faces than female faces (8.1%-20.6% difference in error rate)
  • All classifiers perform better on lighter faces than darker faces (11.8%-19.2% difference in error rate)
  • All classifiers perform worst on darker female faces (20.8%-34.7% error rate)[xvii]

Hypervigilance and the Consequences of Surveillance

Surveillance and privacy are tied to the representation of one’s body, viewership by authority figures and subsequent sexual harassment or discrimination. Underlying theories about gendered perceptions of surveillance is the unwanted male gaze, and the idea that surveillance is often designed and utilised by men.[xviii]

“Usually men sit comfortably in control rooms where they monitor unsuspecting women—and others—from afar. Studies confirm that at least one in 10 women are watched by control room operators for voyeuristic reasons alone”.[xix]

Gendered perceptions of CCTV and building security may be affected by the use of other types of cameras in public, such as personal cell phones. One author notes how at her University people would take pictures of female students without their awareness or consent and post them on a website to be rated for sex appeal.[xx]

Those who are targeted for their gender, gender expression, or sexual identity may be resistant to surveillance. The use of content-monitoring software, exam-proctoring technologies, and other digital surveillance further threatens the privacy of those who have historically been institutionally targeted.

Certain Groups of Gender-Minorities Are Oversurveilled

Heavily surveilled spaces and jobs, such as retail environments, and casinos and strip clubs are often heavily surveilled and mainly staffed by female attendants and waitresses. A study on the surveillance of waitresses in casino’s found that they were positioned as objects of desire for patrons but kept as subordinates to the predominantly male bartending staff and management.[xxi]

Other roles such as nurses involve heavy monitoring with key card scanners for accessing doors and supplies, while domestic workers such as nannies are increasingly surveilled in their employers home.[xxii] When data from hospital key cards have been analysed to track movements of people and supplies, they are almost exclusively used to monitor nurses and patients and not physicians or management.[xxiii]

Amongst call centre employees, females were more likely to feel that there was excessive collection of personal information from CCTV monitoring. Proximity and visibility to senior management in the layout of their workplaces was also an anxiety inducing feature for women specifically.[xxiv]
